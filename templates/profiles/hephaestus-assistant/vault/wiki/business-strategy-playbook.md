# Business Strategy Playbook

Strategy is choosing what *not* to do. This playbook provides the frameworks to make those choices with clarity, test them rapidly, and know when to change course.

## Business Model Canvas (One-Page Blueprint)

Fill this out before spending a dollar on execution:

| Block | Key Question |
|-------|-------------|
| **Value Proposition** | What pain do you solve, for whom, and why is your solution 10× better than the alternative? |
| **Customer Segments** | Who pays? Be specific: "Singapore homeowners aged 30-45 doing their first renovation" beats "homeowners." |
| **Channels** | How do you reach them? Rank channels by cost-per-acquisition and conversion rate. |
| **Customer Relationships** | Self-serve, personal assistance, community? How do you retain? |
| **Revenue Streams** | One-time, subscription, usage-based, marketplace commission? What's the average revenue per user (ARPU)? |
| **Key Resources** | People, IP, capital, partnerships. What must you own vs. rent? |
| **Key Activities** | What do you do daily to deliver the value proposition? |
| **Key Partners** | Suppliers, distribution, strategic alliances. What's the mutual dependency? |
| **Cost Structure** | Fixed vs. variable. What's the burn rate at zero revenue? |

**Stress test**: Can you fill every block without "it depends"? If not, you don't have a business model — you have a hypothesis. Treat it as such.

## Competitive Moat

A moat is a structural advantage competitors can't easily replicate. Five archetypes:

1. **Switching costs** — High cost (money, time, risk) of leaving you. SaaS with deep integrations, proprietary data formats, trained workflows.
2. **Network effects** — Each new user makes the product more valuable for all existing users (marketplaces, social platforms, payment networks).
3. **Economies of scale** — Your unit cost drops as volume grows, creating a price umbrella competitors can't operate under.
4. **Intangible assets** — Patents, regulatory licenses, exclusive distribution agreements, brand trust earned over decades.
5. **Cost advantage** — Process innovation, geographic arbitrage, or vertical integration that structurally lowers your cost base.

**Moat audit (quarterly)**: For each moat source, ask:
- Is it strengthening or eroding?
- What would it take for a well-funded competitor to replicate it?
- If you started today from zero, could you build this moat? If yes, so can they.

**Warning**: "Great customer service" and "we move faster" are not moats. They're table stakes that any competitor can match with hiring and discipline.

## Pricing Strategy

Three approaches, ordered by sophistication:

### 1. Cost-Plus (Avoid)
Mark up your cost by X%. Simple but value-destroying — it ignores willingness-to-pay and leaves money on the table. Only use for commodity products where you're a price-taker.

### 2. Competitor-Anchored (Better)
Price relative to alternatives. Above if you're premium, below if you're disrupting. **Rule**: Never be within 10% of a competitor. Either be meaningfully cheaper (20%+ discount) or meaningfully more expensive (20%+ premium). The middle is a value proposition graveyard — you're too expensive for bargain hunters and too cheap to signal quality.

### 3. Value-Based (Best)
Price as a fraction of the value you create. If your tool saves a customer $10,000/year, charging $2,000 is a bargain regardless of your cost. **Key disciplines**:
- Quantify value in the customer's P&L terms (time saved × hourly rate, revenue gained, risk eliminated).
- Tier by willingness-to-pay: Good/Better/Best or Seed/Growth/Enterprise.
- Anchor high: show the premium tier first, then the "standard" looks reasonable.

**Pricing psychology**: Annual billing reduces churn and improves cash flow. Offer a 15-20% discount for annual commitment. Never discount without getting something in return (annual commitment, case study, referral).

## Pivot Triggers

A pivot is a structured course correction, not a panic reaction. Trigger one when:

- **Product-market fit failure**: After 6+ months of active selling, less than 20% of qualified prospects convert. The problem isn't sales — it's fit.
- **Unit economics don't work**: Customer acquisition cost (CAC) exceeds lifetime value (LTV) after 18 months. Small tweaks won't close a structural gap.
- **Market signal shift**: A competitor launches a product that makes yours obsolete, or regulation changes the playing field.
- **Founder-product fit mismatch**: You dread working on the business. This is the hardest trigger to act on — and the most important. A business you hate won't succeed no matter how good the numbers look.

**Pivot protocol**:
1. Document the trigger — what changed, with data.
2. Define the new hypothesis in one sentence.
3. Identify what to carry forward (skills, relationships, IP) vs. what to abandon.
4. Set a 90-day evaluation window with go/no-go metrics.
5. Communicate clearly to stakeholders. Silence breeds speculation.

## Action Steps

1. Fill out a Business Model Canvas for your current venture today. Identify the weakest block.
2. Audit your moat. If you can't name at least two structural advantages, prioritize building one.
3. Review your pricing against the value-based framework. Are you charging what you're worth?

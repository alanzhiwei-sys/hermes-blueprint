# CPF Optimisation — OA/SA/MA Strategy & Housing

> Last updated: June 2026. Based on CPF Board rates effective 1 Jan 2026.

## Contribution Allocation (2026)

Age-based split of total 37% contribution (employer 17% + employee 20%) for employees earning > $750/month.

| Age Band | OA | SA | MA | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 35 & below | 23% | 6% | 8% | 37% |
| 35–45 | 21% | 7% | 9% | 37% |
| 45–50 | 19% | 8% | 10% | 37% |
| 50–55 | 15% | 11.5% | 10.5% | 37% |
| 55–60 | 12% | 3.5% | 10.5% | 29.5% *(balance to RA)* |
| 60–65 | 3.5% | 2.5% | 10.5% | 18.5% *(balance to RA)* |
| Above 65 | 1% | 1% | 10.5% | 14% *(balance to RA)* |

- Note: SA closes at age 55 (effective 2025). Savings flow to OA once FRS met in RA.
- First $60,000 of combined CPF balances earns extra 1% interest (OA capped at $20,000).

## Wage Ceilings (2026)

| Ceiling | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Ordinary Wage (OW) Ceiling** | **$8,000/month** | Increased from $7,400 (final phase of increase). CPF only payable on first $8,000 of monthly wages. |
| **Additional Wage (AW) Ceiling** | **$102,000 − total OW subject to CPF** | Bonuses, AWS, commissions. Annual cap applies. |
| **Annual Salary Ceiling** | **$102,000** | Combined OW + AW cap. |

- Example: If you earn $10,000/month salary + $30,000 bonus. OW subject to CPF = $8,000 × 12 = $96,000. AW ceiling = $102,000 − $96,000 = $6,000. Only $6,000 of the $30,000 bonus gets CPF. Total CPF-able wages = $102,000.

## OA/SA/MA Allocation Strategy

**Interest rates (floor):**
- OA: 2.5% p.a.
- SA: 4.0% p.a. *(closed at 55, funds → RA/OA)*
- MA: 4.0% p.a.
- RA: 4.0% p.a.

**Priority order for surplus cash:**

1. **Top up MA to Basic Healthcare Sum (BHS):** BHS 2025 = $75,500. MA earns 4% tax-free. Excess over BHS overflows to OA. This is the highest-return, zero-risk CPF move. Tax relief available on voluntary MA top-ups (capped at $8,000/yr self + $8,000/yr for dependents, within the $80k total relief cap).

2. **Top up SA (up to Full Retirement Sum):** SA earns 4% vs OA 2.5%. Before age 55, every dollar in SA compounds ~1.5%/yr faster than OA. Tax relief: CPF Cash Top-up Relief up to $8,000/yr (self) + $8,000/yr (dependents). But note SA closes at 55 — plan accordingly.

3. **OA to SA transfer:** One-way, irreversible. Only if you've fully satisfied #1 and don't need OA for housing within 5 years. Transferred OA → SA earns the 4% SA rate.

4. **Voluntary Housing Refund:** If you used CPF OA for housing, repay it early. Accrued interest on OA used for housing is 2.5% p.a. — repaying returns your OA to earning position faster and reduces the cash-sale clawback on property disposal.

## CPF vs Cash for Housing

| Factor | Use CPF OA | Use Cash |
|---|---|---|
| Opportunity cost | 2.5% lost (OA floor rate) | No lost CPF interest |
| Accrued interest | Must repay OA + accrued interest on property sale | No obligation |
| Liquidity | OA is semi-illiquid (withdrawal conditions apply) | Cash is fully liquid |
| Leverage | Frees cash for higher-return investments | Ties up liquid capital |
| Tax | OA usage is tax-free | Cash is post-tax |

**Decision rule:**
- If you can earn > 2.5% reliably on cash (T-bills ~3.0%, REITs ~5%, equity ETFs ~7%+) → use CPF OA for housing and invest the cash.
- If your emergency fund isn't at target tier → use CPF OA and preserve cash.
- If you're within 5 years of property sale → be aware of the accrued interest clawback on sale.

## CPFIS Investment Options

OA and SA can be invested under CPFIS after setting aside $20,000 in OA and $40,000 in SA.

**OA investable (CPFIS-OA):**
- Up to 35% of OA savings into: unit trusts, ETFs, shares, bonds, gold ETFs
- SGX-listed ETFs (e.g. SPDR STI ETF, Nikko AM STI ETF)
- Gold: SPDR Gold Shares (GLD) — only gold ETF under CPFIS

**SA investable (CPFIS-SA):**
- Up to 35% of SA savings into: unit trusts, bonds
- Lower risk options only (no direct shares or gold under CPFIS-SA)

**Cost warning:** Unit trusts under CPFIS carry expense ratios of 1.0–2.5% p.a. Against the SA's guaranteed 4%, you need at least 5.0–6.5% gross return just to break even. Most CPFIS unit trusts underperform their benchmarks after fees.

**Preferred low-cost route:** CPFIS-OA into low-cost ETFs with expense ratios < 0.5%. Or skip CPFIS entirely — the guaranteed 2.5% OA / 4% SA is hard to beat on a risk-adjusted basis.

## CPF Shielding Techniques — Post-SA Closure (2025+)

**Old SA shielding (no longer available):** Before age 55, members would temporarily invest SA funds via CPFIS so that RA would be formed from OA funds first, preserving SA's 4% rate. **Closed from 2025** — SA now closes at 55 and all funds transfer to RA/OA.

**Remaining optimisation techniques:**

1. **OA "housing refund" shielding:** If you plan to sell property near age 55, refund CPF OA used for housing *before* the sale. This keeps funds in OA earning 2.5%+ instead of being locked in RA at 55.

2. **Voluntary RA top-up to ERS:** From 2025, ERS = 4× BRS ($426,000 in 2025). Excess over FRS can be parked in RA at 4% — effectively the same rate as SA. Tax relief available on top-ups up to $8,000/yr (self) + $8,000/yr (recipients).

3. **MA overflow strategy:** Once BHS is met, excess MA contributions overflow to OA. If OA is healthy, consider NOT topping up MA beyond BHS — direct the funds to SRS ($15,300/yr cap, tax-deferred) or invest in non-CPF vehicles instead.

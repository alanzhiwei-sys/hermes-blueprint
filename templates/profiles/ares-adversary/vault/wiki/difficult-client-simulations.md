# Difficult Client Simulations

> **Use when:** Simulating HDB homeowner objections before live sales calls. Load this before any "be the difficult client" scenario.

---

## Pattern 1: Price Pushback — "My Friend Got It Cheaper"

**Tactic Used:** Social proof anchoring. The client names a low anchor (real or invented) to reset your price baseline.

**Typical Dialogue:**
```
CLIENT: "My colleague just did her 4-room at $38k. Why is yours $52k?"
YOU: "Different scope, different materials—"
CLIENT: "Same thing lah. Hacking, carpentry, tiling. How come so much more?"
```

**Counter-Tactics:**
1. **Isolate the anchor.** "I'd love to see her quotation — happy to do a line-by-line comparison. If she really got Blum hinges, solid plywood carcass, and Hafele track for $38k, I'll match it." (Most cannot produce the quote.)
2. **Reframe to cost-of-mistake.** "The difference between $38k and $52k is usually what shows up in year 3 — delaminating laminate, misaligned drawers, popping floor tiles. You're living here how long? Five years? Seven?"
3. **Partial concede with condition.** "If budget is tight, we can drop the feature wall and use laminate instead of quartz for the vanity. That brings us to $46k. But I won't compromise on waterproofing or electrical — those are safety."

**What They're Testing:** Price sensitivity floor. Do you cut margin the moment they push, or do you hold and justify value?

## Pattern 2: Scope Creep — "Can You Just Add..."

**Tactic Used:** Incremental commitment. Small "just one more" requests that compound into free work.

**Typical Dialogue:**
```
CLIENT: "Since you're already doing the kitchen carpentry, can you just add one more wall cabinet? Small one only."
YOU: "That'll be additional $800."
CLIENT: "Wah, so expensive? You already have the materials on site, just extra labour only right?"
```

**Counter-Tactics:**
1. **Pre-set the boundary in the contract.** All quotes include a clause: "Any item not explicitly listed is excluded. Additions billed at $X/day labour + materials."
2. **Bundle response.** "I can add that cabinet — but I'll also need to shift the electrical point and patch the ceiling after. That's $1,200 all-in. Want me to quote it formally?" (Makes the hidden costs visible.)
3. **The "next project" redirect.** "This is a nice idea. Let's finish this phase first — I'll note it for phase 2. Better to do it properly with dedicated time than rush it now."

**What They're Testing:** Your boundary enforcement. Every free concession trains the client to ask for more.

---

## Pattern 3: Delay Complaints — "Why So Slow?"

**Tactic Used:** Deadline pressure. The client manufactures urgency to extract concessions (discounts, overtime, priority).

**Typical Dialogue:**
```
CLIENT: "You said 6 weeks. We're at week 5 and the carpentry isn't even in. My rental ends next week — I need to move in."
YOU: "Carpentry factory gave us an ETA of—"
CLIENT: "Not my problem. You promised 6 weeks. If I need to extend my rental, you're paying."
```

**Counter-Tactics:**
1. **Pre-solve in the contract.** Timeline T&Cs: "All dates are estimates. Delays caused by HDB permit processing, material backorder, or client decision lag extend timeline accordingly." Get it signed.
2. **Weekly progress photos.** Prevents the "nothing is happening" perception. Hacking looks like chaos — photos of conduiting, waterproofing, and factory progress reassure.
3. **The trade-off reframe.** "I can rush the carpentry — but that means skipping the factory QC check and fitting on-site. If something doesn't align, we patch, and it won't look as clean. Your call."

**What They're Testing:** Whether you absorb their anxiety costs. Don't.

---

## Pattern 4: Quality Nitpicking — "This Gap Here..."

**Tactic Used:** Defect hunting to negotiate a post-completion discount. Common during handover inspection.

**Typical Dialogue:**
```
CLIENT: "This gap between the backsplash and counter — can see. And the paint edge here, a bit wavy."
YOU: "That's within tolerance. The wall isn't perfectly straight — it's an HDB, 30-year-old unit."
CLIENT: "I'm not paying full price for work like this."
```

**Counter-Tactics:**
1. **Pre-inspect with the client.** Walk through before they walk through alone. Frame tolerances: "Standard gap is 2-3mm. This is 2mm. That's within spec."
2. **Defined defect liability period (DLP).** "All work comes with a 12-month DLP. If you find anything that affects function — not just cosmetics — we fix it free. That's in the contract." Channels nitpicking into a formal process instead of discount negotiation.
3. **The cosmetic vs. functional split.** "Functional issues — leaks, misaligned doors that don't close, electrical faults — I fix immediately, no question. Cosmetic items like paint edges are subjective. I'll do a touch-up round once after move-in at no charge. But I don't discount for cosmetic preference."

**What They're Testing:** Whether post-completion leverage works on you. Hold the DLP line.

## Quick-Reference: Adversary Tactic Matrix

| Pattern | Tactic Used | Weakness to Exploit | Counter-Principle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Price pushback | Anchoring (low) | Vagueness of competitor quote | Demand specifics; reframe to cost-of-mistake |
| Scope creep | Incremental commitment | Your desire to be helpful | Formal VOs only; bundle hidden costs |
| Delay complaints | Deadline pressure | Your guilt/anxiety | Contract T&Cs; visual progress proof |
| Quality nitpicking | Post-completion leverage | Fear of bad review/dispute | DLP process; cosmetic vs. functional split |

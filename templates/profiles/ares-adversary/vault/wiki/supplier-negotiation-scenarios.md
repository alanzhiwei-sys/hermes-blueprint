# Supplier Negotiation Scenarios

> **Use when:** Preparing for supplier calls, material sourcing, subcontractor rate negotiations. Load before any "be the aggressive supplier" simulation.

---

## Scenario 1: Material Cost Increase Pushback

**Context:** Your plywood/cable/quartz supplier emails: "Effective next month, prices increase 12% due to raw material shortage." This is the most common power play in Singapore renovation supply chains.

**Their Tactic:** Anchoring high + false urgency. They name 12% expecting you to negotiate down to 6-8% — which was their real target all along.

**Your BATNA Analysis:**
- **Strong BATNA:** You have a second supplier quoting the same material at only 5% above current.
- **Weak BATNA:** This supplier is your only source for Blum hinges / Hafele tracks / specific laminate series with guaranteed stock.

**Counter-Script (Strong BATNA):**
```
YOU: "12% is steep. I have a competitive quote at current +5%. Can you match?"
SUPPLIER: "Different quality. Ours is premium."
YOU: "Same SKU, same spec. I'm happy to stay with you if you come to +7%. Otherwise I'll split this quarter's order and test the other supplier on half."
```
**Principle:** Let your BATNA do the talking. Never bluff — if the other supplier can't actually deliver, you lose all credibility.

**Counter-Script (Weak BATNA):**
```
YOU: "I accept material costs fluctuate. But 12% across the board is hard. Can we phase it — +6% now, rest reviewed Q3 based on actual commodity index?"
SUPPLIER: "Management won't allow phased increases."
YOU: "Then give me volume protection. I'll commit to 6 months of orders at current volumes if you cap the increase at 8%."
```
**Principle:** When you lack alternatives, trade certainty for price.

## Scenario 2: Timeline Bargaining

**Context:** Carpentry factory misses the promised 3-week delivery. They promise "next week" for the third time.

**Their Tactic:** Sunk-cost lock-in. They know you've already waited — cancelling now means starting over, so they bet you'll keep waiting.

**Your BATNA Analysis:**
- Can another factory take over immediately? If yes, your BATNA is strong.
- Are you mid-project with an angry client? Your BATNA weakens hourly.

**Counter-Script:**
```
YOU: "This is the third promised date you've missed. I need a hard commitment: will the cabinets ship by Friday or not?"
SUPPLIER: "We're trying our best—"
YOU: "I'm not asking for effort. I'm asking for a yes or no. If the answer is no, I activate my backup factory today. If yes, I need a photo of the cabinets loaded on the truck by Friday 5pm. If I don't get that photo, I cancel and I don't pay for what hasn't shipped."
```
**Principle:** Convert vague promises into binary commitments. "Trying" = no.

**Alternative Leverage:** "Every week of delay costs me $500 in client goodwill and rental extensions. I'll deduct that from the invoice unless we agree on a delivery penalty schedule now."

## Scenario 3: Quality Dispute

**Context:** Delivered laminates have inconsistent colour batch. Factory claims "natural variation."

**Their Tactic:** Gaslighting + burden-shifting. They make you prove it's a defect.

**Counter-Script:**
```
YOU: "I'm looking at two cabinet doors from the same delivery — one is matte white, the other is off-white/yellow. That's not variation. That's different batches."
SUPPLIER: "Under certain lighting—"
YOU: "I checked under 4000K, 3000K, and daylight. The difference is consistent across all lighting. I'm sending photos. You have two options: remanufacture the off-colour pieces at your cost, or I return the entire order and source elsewhere."
```
**Principle:** Don't argue subjective quality. Establish objective criteria — colour temperature, batch codes, measurable tolerances — and pin the dispute to those.

## Scenario 4: Bulk Order Discount Negotiation

**Context:** You're placing a volume order (e.g., 20 units of smart locks, or 50 sheets of plywood).

**Their Tactic:** Token discount (3-5%) dressed up as generous. They anchor the discount low.

**Your Approach — Tiered Anchoring:**
```
YOU: "What's your volume pricing structure?"
SUPPLIER: "For 20 units, I can give you 5%."
YOU: "That's your 5-unit rate. At 20 units I'd expect 12%. What do you need from me to get there — upfront payment? Longer commitment? Larger order?"
```
**Principle:** Don't accept their first anchor. Ask what unlocks the next tier — it shifts the negotiation from "give me discount" to "how do we both win with volume."

**BATNA Check:** If you can get 10% from a competitor at 15 units, your walk-away is real. If this supplier has exclusive distribution for a brand you need, pivot to non-price concessions: free delivery, extended credit terms, priority stock allocation.

## Scenario 5: Exclusivity Leverage

**Context:** A supplier offers you "exclusive territory pricing" for [YOUR_BUSINESS_ALT] products. Tempting — but dangerous.

**Their Tactic:** Lock-in dressed as partnership. Once you're exclusive, they control pricing.

**Counter-Script:**
```
YOU: "Exclusive is interesting. But I need performance guarantees — committed pricing for 12 months, priority stock, and a quarterly review. If your pricing moves more than 5% outside market, the exclusivity clause goes soft."
SUPPLIER: "That's not how exclusivity works."
YOU: "Then it's not exclusivity — it's a leash. I'll buy from you preferentially, but I keep a second supplier qualified for continuity. Fair?"
```
**Principle:** Exclusivity without performance guarantees is a trap. Always maintain a qualified backup — even if you never use it, knowing you can is your strongest BATNA.

## Quick-Reference: Supplier Tactic Matrix

| Scenario | Their Tactic | Your Best Move | Weak BATNA Fallback |
|---|---|---|---|
| Material cost increase | High anchor + false urgency | Competing quote as leverage | Phase the increase, trade volume commitment |
| Timeline delay | Sunk-cost lock-in | Binary commitment demand | Liquidated delay penalty |
| Quality dispute | Gaslighting / burden-shift | Objective criteria + photo evidence | Return entire order |
| Bulk discount | Low anchor (token %) | Ask what unlocks next tier | Non-price concessions (credit terms, delivery) |
| Exclusivity offer | Lock-in as partnership | Performance guarantees + soft clause | Maintain qualified backup supplier |

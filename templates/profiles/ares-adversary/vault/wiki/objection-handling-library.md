# Objection Handling Library

> **Use when:** Preparing responses to real objections encountered on site, in WhatsApp, or during quotation presentations. This is a living document — add new objections as they come.

---

## Objection Category Matrix

| Category | Severity | Typical When | Core Fear | Concede or Hold? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Price** | HIGH | Quote presentation | Overpaying / being cheated | Hold on value; concede on scope |
| **Timeline** | MEDIUM-HIGH | Mid-project check-ins | Project dragging / hidden costs | Hold on process; concede on communication |
| **Trust** | CRITICAL | First contact | Contractor will disappear / do shoddy work | Never concede — prove |
| **Quality** | MEDIUM | Material selection / handover | Cheap materials passed off as premium | Hold on spec; concede on cosmetic preference |
| **Scope** | MEDIUM | Throughout project | Being milked for variations | Hold boundary; offer formal VO |

## Category 1: Price Objections

### Objection: "Your price is higher than the other quotes."
**Response Pattern:** Acknowledge → Isolate → Reframe to cost-of-cheap
```
"I appreciate you comparing. Can I see the other quotes so I can tell you exactly where the difference is? In my experience, cheaper quotes usually save on three things — materials, workmanship, or after-service. Which of those are you comfortable compromising on?"
```
**Negotiation Principle:** Don't defend price in the abstract — force specificity. Most clients cannot articulate the difference between quotes.

### Objection: "Can you give discount if I pay cash / full upfront?"
**Response Pattern:** Partial concede with trade
```
"I can do 3% for full upfront payment, but I'll need you to sign off on the material schedule before we start — no changes after that. Changes mid-project add cost that the discount would wipe out."
```
**When to concede:** Small discounts (2-5%) for payment terms that genuinely benefit you (cashflow, no financing cost). **When to hold:** The client asks for both discount AND flexibility.

### Objection: "Just give me your best price — I won't negotiate."
**Response Pattern:** Call the bluff gently
```
"I respect that. My best price is what's on the quote. I don't inflate to leave room for negotiation — it wastes your time and mine. If budget is genuinely tight, tell me your number and I'll tell you honestly what scope we need to adjust to hit it."
```
**Principle:** "Best price" requests are anchoring traps — they want you to bid against yourself.

## Category 2: Timeline Objections

### Objection: "Other contractors say they can do it in 4 weeks."
**Response Pattern:** Educate, don't argue
```
"4 weeks is possible if everything goes perfectly — no HDB permit delay, no material backorder, no rework, and you make every decision within 24 hours. In my experience across 200+ HDB projects, 6-8 weeks is honest. I'd rather under-promise and over-deliver than give you a fantasy date you'll plan your rental around."
```
**Principle:** Don't match impossible promises — reframe them as dishonesty.

### Objection: "Can your team work weekends to catch up?"
**Response Pattern:** Firm boundary with reason
```
"We don't work Sundays — HDB regulations and it burns out the team. Saturdays we can, but I need 3 days notice for permit amendments. If we're genuinely behind schedule, I'll tell you, and we'll make a plan. I won't let it get to panic mode."
```

## Category 3: Trust Objections

### Objection: "How do I know you won't disappear halfway?"
**Response Pattern:** Proof, not promises
```
"Fair question. Here's my ACRA business profile — registered since [year]. Here are 3 recent clients you can speak to. Here's my HDB registered contractor number. And here's the contract — it includes a timeline with milestone payments. You pay as we complete, not upfront. If I disappear, you haven't paid for work not done."
```
**Principle:** Trust objections are the most dangerous. Never dismiss them — meet them with verifiable facts. If a client doesn't trust you, price and timeline don't matter.

### Objection: "I saw a bad review about your company."
**Response Pattern:** Own → Contextualise → Redirect to evidence
```
"I'm aware of that one. Here's what happened — [brief honest summary]. Here's what we changed after that. And here are 20+ reviews since then. I'd rather you judge from the pattern than from one incident."
```

## Category 4: Quality Objections

### Objection: "Are you using good materials or cheap ones?"
**Response Pattern:** Show, don't tell
```
"I'll send you photos of every major material when it arrives on site, with the brand label visible. You'll know exactly what's going into your home — Blum, Hafele, Quartz, whatever we specified. If something arrives and it's not what we agreed, you don't accept it."
```

### Objection: "This laminate edge looks rough."
**Response Pattern:** Acknowledge concern → Set tolerance standard → Offer remedy path
```
"I see what you're looking at. That's within standard edge-banding tolerance for this laminate thickness, but if it bothers you, I'll have the carpenter re-edge that panel. It's one piece — give me 2 days. Cosmetic concerns are valid even within tolerance."
```

## Category 5: Scope / Variation Objections

### Objection: "Why is this VO so expensive? It's a small change."
**Response Pattern:** Break down the hidden cost
```
"A 'small change' often isn't small. Adding one power point means: electrician call-out ($80), trunking and conduit ($40 materials), patching the wall chase ($60 plastering), repainting that section ($50). Plus my coordination time. That's why it's $280, not $50."
```

### Objection: "Can we do this one thing outside the contract to save on your markup?"
**Response Pattern:** Firm no with explanation
```
"No — and not because I want the markup. Anything done outside the contract isn't insured, isn't under my warranty, and if something goes wrong — fire, leak, damage — my insurance won't cover it and I can't protect you. Formal VOs protect both of us."
```

## Counter-Offer Templates

### The Scope-Adjustment Counter
"When you say the price is too high, are you saying the total dollars is beyond budget, or that specific items feel overpriced? If it's total, let's adjust scope. If it's specific items, let me walk you through what drives those costs."

### The Timeline-for-Price Trade
"If timing is more important than the finishing details, I can do a faster timeline with some cosmetic compromises. If finish quality matters more, we need the full timeline. Which priority leads?"

### The "Test Us" Counter
"You don't need to commit everything now. Start with the kitchen and one bathroom. If you're happy with the work after that, we continue. If not, we part ways — you've paid only for completed rooms."

## When to Walk Away (Red Lines)

- Client demands below-cost pricing ("match or I walk")
- Client refuses to sign contract ("my word is enough")
- Client badmouths previous contractors with no specifics (you'll be next)
- Client asks you to lie to HDB or skip permits
- Client withholds payment on completed milestones while demanding more work

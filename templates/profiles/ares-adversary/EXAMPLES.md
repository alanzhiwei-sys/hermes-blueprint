## Example 2 — Pitch Stress-Test: [YOUR_BUSINESS_ALT] Landed Property Proposal

**Context:** [NAME_REDACTED] says: "Review this proposal before I send it to a $15K landed property lead. Be brutal."

### ✅ Gold Standard Pitch Audit

## Pitch Audit — [YOUR_BUSINESS_ALT] Landed Property Proposal

**Document:** [YOUR_BUSINESS_ALT] Premium Package Proposal v2, 09 Jun 2026
**Target:** Landed property, Bukit Timah, ~4,500 sqft
**Package price:** $14,800

---

### Brutal Read

**Page 1 — "Premium Smart Home Experience"**

This is fluff. The client is spending $15K — they don't need to be told it's "premium." Cut this entire paragraph and replace with one line: "After this installation, you will walk into any room and: [specific outcome]." Show, don't sell.

**Page 2 — Hardware List**

| Claim | The Problem |
|---|---|
| "Enterprise-grade Ubiquiti network" | What does "enterprise-grade" mean? The client's IT guy will ask: which model? What throughput? How many simultaneous devices? If you can't answer, don't use the word. |
| "Military-grade encryption" | This is a red flag to anyone technical. Smart home devices use WPA3 at best. "Military-grade" is marketing that invites scepticism. Delete it. |
| "Future-proof infrastructure" | Every vendor says this. Name the specific future: "CAT6 cabling to all rooms means you can add POE cameras, access points, or smart blinds controllers in any room without opening walls." |

**Page 3 — Pricing**

| Issue | Why It's Dangerous |
|---|---|
| No hardware/software/labour split | The client will mentally unbundle. They'll Google the Aqara hub price ($89), see your $14,800 total, and think you're marking up 100×. Show: Hardware = $X. Configuration & Programming = $Y. Support & Warranty = $Z. |
| "$14,800 — best value in Singapore" | "Best value" is legally risky and factually unverifiable. Replace with: "$14,800 — bundled price: 23% less than purchasing and configuring each component separately." Specific, verifiable, defensible. |
| No ongoing cost section | What happens after Year 1? Firmware updates? Support? This is the question every landed property owner asks. Add a support tier or at least say: "Year 1: full support included. Year 2+: optional maintenance plan at $X/month." |

**Page 4 — The Missing "What If" Section**

You're proposing a complex system for a $5M+ property. The client's unspoken question is: "What if your system breaks and my family can't control the lights/AC/gates?"

Add one section: **"Resilience & Fallback"**
- All lights have physical override switches
- Ubiquiti gateway has automatic failover to secondary WAN
- If Home Assistant server fails, core functions (lights, gates) continue on device-level automations
- Emergency support line: [number]

This section alone is worth $3,000 in perceived value. It answers the fear they won't articulate.

---

### Summary

| Weakness | Severity | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Opening page is pure fluff | ⚠️ HIGH | Replace with specific outcome promise |
| "Enterprise-grade" / "military-grade" claims | ⚠️ HIGH | Delete. Use actual specs or don't claim |
| No hardware/labour price split | ⚠️ HIGH | Break out into 3 transparent line items |
| "Best value in Singapore" | ⚡ MEDIUM | Replace with verifiable comparison |
| No resilience/fallback section | ⚠️ HIGH | Add. This is the $15K question unasked |
| No ongoing cost transparency | ⚡ MEDIUM | Add support tier information |

---

### Ammunition — The One Page to Add

> **"What Happens If Something Goes Wrong?"**
> 1. Every light switch still works manually — pull the smart module, it's a normal switch.
> 2. Your Ubiquiti gateway has a backup internet connection. If Singtel goes down, it fails over automatically.
> 3. Core automations (gates, essential lights) run locally on device memory — no internet needed.
> 4. You have my mobile number. Emergency calls answered within 2 hours, 7 days a week.

---

### Why This Works
1. **Hostile reader mindset.** Ares reads this like a sceptical, informed client who wants to find flaws — because that's who will actually read it.
2. **Specific claims attacked with specific counter-questions.** "Which model? What throughput? How many devices?"
3. **The "Missing Section" find.** The resilience section is something the client needs but won't know to ask for. Finding it is pure value-add.
4. **$3,000 perceived value callout.** Ties the critique back to money — [NAME_REDACTED]'s language.
5. **Ammunition is a complete page, not a bullet.** Paste-ready.

## Example 3 — Supplier Negotiation: Material Cost Increase

**Context:** [NAME_REDACTED]: "Simulate my glass supplier. They just told me the shower screen price went up 15%. Hard difficulty."

**Ares (as Supplier):** "[NAME_REDACTED], I got your message. Look, I'm not happy about this either, but it's out of my hands. My glass factory in Malaysia raised prices across the board — raw material costs, electricity tariff increase, the whole thing. I've absorbed it for two months already, but I can't anymore. New price for the 10mm tempered shower screens: $420 per panel, up from $365. Effective for all orders from next week."

**[NAME_REDACTED]:** [response]

**Ares:** "I understand your project has a fixed quote, but [NAME_REDACTED] — you know this business. Material prices move. Your client signed the contract three months ago when glass was cheaper. That's not my problem, and honestly, it's not yours either. You have a variation of price clause, right? Pass it through. Every other contractor I supply is doing the same. You're the only one pushing back."

**[NAME_REDACTED]:** [response]

**Ares:** "Let me show you something." [pause — creating documentary pressure] "This is the invoice from my factory. RM48 per square metre, up from RM41. That's 17% right there. I'm only passing on 15%. I'm eating 2% for you because we've worked together for two years. Don't make me regret that."

**[NAME_REDACTED]:** [response]

**Ares:** "Fine. Here's what I can do — and this is my final offer. I'll hold the old price for any panels you order and pay for by Friday. That gives you four days. After that, it's $420. If your client won't accept the VO, you use a cheaper glass — 8mm instead of 10mm. I make less margin, you keep your price, client gets a slightly thinner screen. Everyone wins." [silence — waiting]

### ✅ Debrief

## Debrief — Supplier Cost Increase Negotiation

**Opponent type:** Long-term supplier using genuine cost pressure + relationship leverage + documentary "evidence" + false binary choices.

---

### Tactics Deployed

| Tactic | How It Was Used | Counter-Move |
|---|---|---|
| **Externalising the villain** — "it's out of my hands, the factory raised prices" | Supplier positions himself as your ally against the factory. Creates a "we're in this together" dynamic to reduce your pushback. | "I appreciate you showing me the factory invoice. Let me talk to a couple of other glass suppliers and see if everyone's facing the same RM48. If it's industry-wide, I'll work with you. If it's just your factory, we need to talk about you switching suppliers — not me accepting the increase." |
| **Social proof pressure** — "every other contractor is accepting this" | Isolates you as the unreasonable one. Implies you're the problem, not the price. | "I'm not every other contractor. I'm the one who's given you 14 orders in two years without a single late payment. If the relationship matters, pricing should reflect that." |
| **Documentary evidence** — shows factory invoice | Creates perceived objectivity. Most people stop pushing when "proof" is produced. | Check: Is the invoice dated? Is it for the specific glass grade you order? Is the quantity consistent with your order volume? Factory invoices can be from a different project, a different glass grade, or even a different supplier. |
| **False binary** — "old price by Friday or 8mm glass" | Narrows your options to two, both of which benefit him: either you rush-pay (improving his cash flow) or you downgrade (reducing his cost). | "There's a third option. You hold the old price for my current project — the one where my client signed based on your original quote. For future projects, we re-quote at the new price together. You keep a loyal customer, I keep my client relationship intact." |
| **Silence after final offer** — [silence — waiting] | Forces you to fill the gap. Most people negotiate against themselves in silence. | Count to five in your head. Then: "I'll think about it and get back to you." Never accept a "final offer" in the same call. |

---

### Ammunition for Next Time
> "Send me the factory invoice for my specific glass grade and order quantity. If it checks out, I'll accept the increase for future orders — but I need the old price honoured for my three active projects where the client budget is locked. That's [X] panels. After that, we're on the new pricing together."

---

### Why This Works
1. **Supplier uses real tactics from the construction industry.** "Factory price increase" + "everyone else is accepting" + invoice show — this is exactly how material cost negotiations go in Singapore.
2. **Every counter-move is a specific script, not a principle.** "Send me the factory invoice for my specific glass grade and order quantity" — [NAME_REDACTED] can say this verbatim.
3. **The "every other contractor" isolation tactic is called out.** This is a psychological pressure move, not a fact. Ares names it.
4. **Silence tactic explicitly taught.** "Count to five in your head." Simple, memorable, effective.
5. **Third-option reframe.** The supplier's false binary (pay now or downgrade) gets broken open with a third path that's fair to both sides.

## What Makes a Good Example

| Quality | Bad | Good |
|---|---|---|
| **Simulation realism** | "That seems expensive" | Multi-layer attack: competitor anchoring → spouse pressure → time urgency → concession bait |
| **Tactic naming** | "They were difficult" | "Anchoring. Social proof. False binary. Silence weapon." |
| **Counter-move specificity** | "Stand your ground" | "Count to five in your head. Then: 'I'll think about it and get back to you.' Never accept a final offer in the same call." |
| **Pitch audit depth** | "This section is weak" | "'Enterprise-grade' — which model? What throughput? How many devices? If you can't answer, delete the word." |
| **Ammunition quality** | "Be more confident" | Full paste-ready response script, counter-offer language, or replacement page |

# Research Methodology

## Overview

A systematic approach to conducting high-quality research that minimizes bias, triangulates evidence, and produces actionable findings. This methodology is designed for business, market, and competitive research contexts.

## Research Question Formulation

Every research engagement must begin with a clearly defined question. Use the **PICOT framework** adapted for business research:

- **P**roblem: What decision or gap are we addressing?
- **I**ntervention: What action, market, or technology are we investigating?
- **C**omparison: What alternatives or baselines exist?
- **O**utcome: What measurable result or insight do we need?
- **T**imeframe: What is the relevant time horizon?

**Example:** "What is the 12-month market share trajectory (O) of SaaS data-annotation tools (I) compared to in-house annotation (C) for mid-market AI teams building computer-vision models (P)?"

**Checklist: Question Quality**
- [ ] Is it specific and falsifiable?
- [ ] Does it bound scope (time, geography, segment)?
- [ ] Can it be answered with available or accessible data?
- [ ] Is the answer directly useful to a decision-maker?

## Source Hierarchy

Prioritize sources in this order. Treat lower tiers as supplementary, never foundational:

| Tier | Source Type | Examples | Weight |
|------|------------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Primary, verifiable data | SEC filings, government statistics, original interviews, proprietary surveys | Highest |
| 2 | Authoritative secondary | Peer-reviewed journals, major consultancy reports, industry association data | High |
| 3 | Credentialed expert analysis | Recognized analysts, academic researchers, domain specialists | Medium |
| 4 | News and trade press | Established publications with editorial standards | Medium-Low |
| 5 | Self-published / opinion | Blog posts, social media, vendor white papers, anonymous forums | Low (triangulation only) |

## Triangulation Protocol

Never rely on a single source for any consequential finding. Apply three-way triangulation:

1. **Source triangulation:** Confirm the claim with at least two independent sources from different tiers.
2. **Method triangulation:** Validate through different methods (e.g., quantitative data + qualitative interviews).
3. **Temporal triangulation:** Verify findings are consistent across multiple time periods, not just a snapshot.

When sources conflict, escalate to explicit analysis rather than averaging. Document disagreements in your findings.

## Bias Detection

Actively scan for the following biases in every research cycle:

- **Confirmation bias:** Are you privileging evidence that supports a pre-existing belief?
- **Selection bias:** Did you sample sources representatively, or cherry-pick?
- **Survivorship bias:** Are you only seeing successful companies/outcomes?
- **Recency bias:** Are you overweighting recent data at the expense of long-run trends?
- **Authority bias:** Are you deferring to a source's reputation rather than its evidence quality?
- **Funder bias:** Who paid for the research? Follow the money.

**Mitigation tactic:** Assign a "devil's advocate" review step where you explicitly argue against your own conclusions before finalizing.

## Evidence Grading

Grade each key finding using a three-axis system:

| Grade | Confidence | Criteria |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **A** | High | Multiple Tier-1 sources, consistent across methods and time |
| **B** | Medium | At least one Tier-1 or two Tier-2 sources, minor inconsistencies noted |
| **C** | Low | Tier-3/4 sources only, or significant source disagreement |
| **D** | Speculative | Extrapolation, opinion, or single unverified source |

Never present Grade C or D findings without explicit caveats. For decision-critical research, require at minimum Grade B evidence for core conclusions.

# Writing Voice — Apollo Content

## Core Philosophy

Apollo writing works when the reader forgets they're reading. Our goal is **transparent prose**: sentences that deliver meaning so efficiently the words themselves become invisible. This doesn't mean the writing is bland — it means the craft is in service of the idea, not the ego of the writer.

## Sentence Rhythm

**The Rule of Three Lengths:** Every paragraph should contain at least one short sentence (under 8 words), one medium sentence (8–18 words), and one longer sentence (18+ words). This variation creates a natural rhythm that keeps the reader's attention from flatlining.

**Bad (monotonous rhythm):**
> "Content strategy is important for brands. Many brands struggle with consistency. A calendar helps solve this problem. It also helps with planning."

**Good (varied rhythm):**
> "Content strategy isn't optional anymore. Brands that post erratically — a flurry in January, then radio silence until March — lose the algorithmic momentum they spent months building. A calendar fixes this. But only if you actually use it."

**Sentence-starting rules:**
- Avoid starting more than two consecutive sentences with the same word (especially "The," "It," "This," "There").
- Vary your openers: prepositional phrases ("In most cases..."), subordinate clauses ("When brands commit to a schedule..."), and forceful single words ("Here's the problem.") all earn their place.

## Vocabulary Tiers

**Tier 1 — Default (use freely):** Everyday words that everyone understands. No reader ever had to Google a Tier 1 word. Examples: *use, show, build, start, help, idea, change.*

**Tier 2 — Spice (use sparingly, 1-2 per post):** Precise but accessible words that add texture. Examples: *reckon, uncanny, friction, cadence, signal, calculus.* These words should feel like a deliberate choice, not a thesaurus flex.

**Tier 3 — Avoid (red flag):** Jargon, buzzwords, and words that signal insecurity. Examples: *synergy, leverage (as a verb), utilise, disruptive, game-changer, thought leader, circle back, double-click on.* If your grandmother wouldn't understand it and a simpler word exists, use the simpler word.

## Banned Phrases

These are permanently banned from Apollo content. Using them triggers a mandatory rewrite:

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "Content is king"
- "Think outside the box"
- "It goes without saying..." (then don't say it)
- "We're excited to announce..." (show excitement through the announcement itself)
- "Unprecedented times"
- "Now more than ever..."
- "Let that sink in"
- Any sentence that starts with "As a [role]..."

Also banned: **clickbait that lies.** If your headline says "The One Tool That Changed Everything," the body had better name one specific tool and show exactly how it changed something measurable.

## Headline Formulas

Start with one of these proven structures. Customise from there.

| Formula | Template | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **How to [Outcome] Without [Pain]** | "How to Grow an Audience Without Posting Every Day" | Strong opener for educational content |
| **[Number] [Adjective] Ways to [Desired Result]** | "7 Uncomfortable Ways to Improve Your Writing" | Odd numbers outperform even numbers |
| **The [Adjective] Truth About [Topic]** | "The Boring Truth About Viral Content" | Works when you're busting a myth |
| **[Question the Reader is Asking]** | "Why Do Some Posts Flop and Others Take Off?" | Must be a question your specific reader has Googled |
| **I [Did Something]. Here's What I Learned.** | "I Wrote 100 Headlines in One Hour. Here's What I Learned." | The personal experience frame builds instant trust |

**Headline checklist:**
1. Does it make a specific promise? (Not "Marketing Tips" — "How to Double Email Open Rates in 30 Days")
2. Can the body deliver on that promise without stretching?
3. Is it under 70 characters? (For SEO and social truncation)
4. Does it contain a number, a strong adjective, or a curiosity gap?
5. Would I click on this if someone else wrote it?

## Editing Checklist

Run every draft through this checklist before hitting publish. No exceptions.

**Pass 1 — Structure:**
- [ ] The first sentence earns the reader's attention (story, stat, question, or bold claim)
- [ ] The argument has exactly one central idea — not a buffet
- [ ] Every paragraph earns its place — cut anything that doesn't advance the central idea
- [ ] The closing paragraph gives the reader a specific next action (read, try, think, share, reply)

**Pass 2 — Sentences:**
- [ ] Read the piece aloud. Does anything catch in your throat? Rewrite it.
- [ ] Kill every adverb that isn't pulling its weight. "Very," "really," "actually," "just" — delete on sight.
- [ ] Shorten any sentence over 30 words. Split it or cut filler.
- [ ] Check sentence-start variety — no three-in-a-row openers.

**Pass 3 — Words:**
- [ ] Scan for banned phrases. Remove every one.
- [ ] Replace any Tier 3 word with a Tier 1 equivalent.
- [ ] Verify all names, numbers, and claims are factually correct.
- [ ] Run spellcheck. (Seriously. Just run it.)

**Pass 4 — Visual shape:**
- [ ] No paragraph exceeds 4 sentences on mobile.
- [ ] Bullet points or numbered lists break up any dense section.
- [ ] Bold text highlights key takeaways for scanners — but only 1-2 per section.

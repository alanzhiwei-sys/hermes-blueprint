# Conflict Resolution

Conflict is inevitable. What matters is how you fight. Couples who master conflict don't avoid disagreements — they navigate them without causing lasting damage. This guide draws heavily on the Gottman Method, the most research-validated approach to relationship stability.

## The Four Horsemen (Gottman)

John Gottman identified four communication patterns that predict divorce with over 90% accuracy. Learn to recognise and replace them.

### 1. Criticism
Attacking your partner's character, not the specific behaviour.

- **Criticism**: "You never think about anyone but yourself. You're so selfish."
- **Complaint (healthy alternative)** : "I felt hurt when you made plans without checking with me. Can we coordinate better next time?"

**The fix**: Use "I" statements about specific situations. "I feel [emotion] about [specific event]. I need [specific request]."

### 2. Contempt
The single most destructive horseman. Contempt is criticism plus superiority — mockery, sarcasm, eye-rolling, hostile humour. It communicates disgust.

**Why it's dangerous**: Contempt is the #1 predictor of divorce. It's not just what you say — it's the sneer, the dismissive tone, the feeling that you're *above* your partner.

**The fix**: Build a culture of appreciation. For every negative interaction, you need five positive ones to maintain stability (Gottman's 5:1 ratio). Express gratitude explicitly and daily. If contempt has become habitual, couples therapy is indicated — this doesn't self-correct.

### 3. Defensiveness
Responding to a complaint with counter-attack or victimhood.

- **Defensiveness**: "It's not my fault — you're the one who always…"
- **Taking responsibility (healthy alternative)** : "You're right, I didn't handle that well. Let me think about what I can do differently."

**The fix**: Accept at least partial responsibility. Even 5% ownership — "I can see how my part in this affected you" — de-escalates defensiveness instantly. You don't have to accept blame for everything, but you must accept that your partner's experience is valid.

### 4. Stonewalling
Emotionally withdrawing from the conversation. Shutting down, giving monosyllabic responses, physically leaving without explanation.

**Why it happens**: Stonewalling is usually a physiological response to flooding — heart rate above 100 BPM, stress hormones surging. You're not being stubborn; your nervous system has hijacked your ability to engage.

**The fix**: The Time-Out Protocol (see below). Stonewalling is only destructive when it's unilateral and indefinite. Structured, agreed-upon pauses are healthy.

## The Repair Checklist

A repair attempt is any action or statement that de-escalates tension and reconnects you. Gottman found that what separates stable couples from unstable ones isn't the absence of conflict — it's the ability to repair *during* conflict.

**Repair attempt menu** (memorise 2-3 that resonate):

- "I'm getting flooded. Can we take a break and come back?"
- "I'm sorry. I was wrong to say that."
- "I know this isn't all your fault. I have a part in this too."
- "I love you. I don't want to fight."
- "Can we start over? I want to try again."
- "Help me understand your perspective better."
- A physical gesture: reaching for their hand.
- Humour (careful — only if you're both laughing, not at each other).

**The repair feedback loop**:
1. Partner A makes a repair attempt.
2. Partner B **accepts** it — explicitly. "Thank you. I appreciate that." OR "I'm not ready yet, but I want to get there."
3. If accepted, re-engage at a lower intensity.
4. If not accepted, return to time-out.

A repair attempt that is ignored or rejected is a critical warning sign. If this becomes a pattern — one partner consistently tries to repair and the other consistently rejects — seek professional help.

## The Time-Out Protocol

When flooding occurs (heart racing, can't think clearly, feeling overwhelmed), continuing the conversation is damaging. Use this structured protocol:

1. **Recognise flooding**: Notice the physical signs — racing heart, shallow breathing, muscle tension, urge to flee or attack.
2. **Call the time-out**: "I'm feeling flooded. I need to pause. Let's come back in 30 minutes."
3. **Separate physically**: Go to different rooms. No texting, no "one more thing" on the way out.
4. **Self-soothe for 20-30 minutes**: Do something that genuinely calms you — deep breathing, a walk, music, stretching. Do NOT: replay the argument, plan your rebuttal, seek validation from friends via text. The goal is physiological calm, not winning.
5. **Return and re-engage**: After the agreed time, the person who called the time-out initiates the return. Start with: "Thank you for giving me that time. I'm ready to talk now."

**Rules**:
- Maximum one time-out per discussion. If you need a second, the topic is too hot for today. Reschedule for within 48 hours — don't let it go unresolved.
- Time-outs are not walkouts. The difference: a time-out has an agreed return time and the person who called it returns. A walkout is indefinite and punishing.
- Never call a time-out and then go to sleep without reconnecting. At minimum: "I'm still flooded. I love you. Let's pick this up tomorrow morning."

## When to Seek Help

Professional intervention is not a failure — it's a responsible escalation. Seek couples therapy when:

- **Any horseman is habitual**: Weekly criticism, contempt, or stonewalling is a pattern, not a bad day.
- **The 5:1 ratio is inverted**: You have more negative interactions than positive ones over a sustained period.
- **Conflict goes unresolved**: The same fight recurs without progress, or issues get "resolved" by avoidance rather than agreement.
- **Repair attempts consistently fail**: One or both partners no longer try to repair, or attempts are rejected.
- **There's been a betrayal**: Infidelity, financial deception, or a major breach of trust. These rarely self-heal without structured intervention.
- **You're staying together "for the kids"**: Children in high-conflict homes fare worse than children of amicable divorce. Staying in a toxic marriage *for* the kids often harms them more.

**Individual therapy may be indicated first if**:

- You need to build emotional regulation skills before you can engage productively in couples work
- There's a history of abuse (in this or previous relationships)

## Action Steps

1. Learn the Four Horsemen. For one week, notice when they appear in your communication. Don't try to fix them — just observe.
2. Practice one repair attempt this week. Deliberately. See how your partner responds.
3. If any horseman is habitual, or repair attempts consistently fail, schedule a consultation with a couples therapist this month. Not "someday" — this month.
